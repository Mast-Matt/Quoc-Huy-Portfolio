## The problem

Vietnam's motorcycle aftermarket is huge but fragmented — buyers need to know which parts fit which bikes, what financing looks like, and they want to pay online without friction. Most existing storefronts solve at most one of those.

## My role

Full-stack, in a four-person student team running a hybrid Waterfall–Scrum process. I led the front-end and shared back-end work.

## What we built

- **Storefront** in Next.js + TailwindCSS, with MongoDB-backed catalog, browsing by bike model, and faceted filters.
- **Part compatibility checker** — pick your bike, the catalog filters to parts known to fit. Saves the buyer the back-and-forth of confirming with a mechanic.
- **Interest-rate calculator** for installment plans — useful in a market where financed purchases are common.
- **PayPal Sandbox checkout** over TLS 1.3 for the payment flow.

## Process

Agile-ish: GitHub for source + PR review, Trello for the backlog, sprint retrospectives every two weeks. The hybrid Waterfall–Scrum part was real — the scope was fixed upfront for the academic deliverable, but we ran iterative sprints inside that envelope.

## What I'd do differently

Compatibility data was the hardest thing in the project, and we treated it as a catalog problem. It's really a graph problem — bikes have variants, parts have revisions, "fits" is a many-to-many with conditions. A small graph schema would have saved a lot of `if`-statements later.
